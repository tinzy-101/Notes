1.1 Temperature and Thermal Equilibrium

Temperature 

	--> temp = quantity of what is measured w/ thermometer
	--> proportional to KE of translation 
	--> diff in temp --> heat transfer

Thermal Equilibrium 

	--> thermal equilibrium = 2 objects in close contact but no net energy transfer = same temp
	--> zeroth law of thermodynamics
	--> systems need to be macroscopic = have many molecules, so sys can be in thermal equilbiirum with itself in some molecules

1.2 Thermometers and Temperature Scales

	--> any physical property that depends on temp can be use as basis of thermometer 
		--> electrical resistance, color, IR emission
	--> scales: celsius, fahrenheit, kelvin
		--> C = 5/9*(F-32)
		--> K = C + 273.15
		--> absolute temp scale =  zero point is absolute zero (e.g. kelvin)
	--> kelvin is SI + defined based on triple point (when ice + liquid + vapor exist together) 
		--> T = P / TP * T(TP) --> TP = triple point

1.3 Thermal Expansion

	--> as air heated, expands + rises (same for liquids, gases, and solids)
	--> increase temp = increase KE = increase avg distance b/w molecules
	--> linear thermal expansion: dL/dT = alpha * L
		--> dL/dT = instantaneous change in length per temp 
		--> L = length
		--> alpha = coefficient of linear expansion (1/degrees c or 1/kelvin), different for each solid

Thermal Expansion in 2 and 3 Dimensions 

	--> things expand proportionally
		--> delta(A) = 2*alpha*A*delta(T)
			--> A= area, alpha = coefficient of linear expansion, T = temp 
		--> delta(V) = beta*V*delta(T)
			--> beta = coefficient of volume expansion
	--> generally: increase temp, expand (water opposite)

1.4 Heat transfer, Specific Heat. and Calorimetry

	--> heat = energy transfer due to temp difference + changes temp of an object
	--> internal energy can be increased/decrease by heat transfer

Internal Energy and Heat 

	--> thermal system has internal energy = sum of microscopic energies of system
	--> heat unit = J or cal (amt to change temp of 1.00 gram of water by 1.00 degree C)

Mechanical Equivalent of Heat 

	--> can also change temp by doing work (transfer heat in/out of sys) 
	--> 1.000 kcal = 4186 J
	--> sys has well-defined internal nrg, but can't say it has heat/work content 
		--> heat and work not state variables 
	--> increase internal nrg doesn't necessarily mean increase temp

Temperature Change and Heat Capacity 

	--> heat transfer directly proportional to change in temp + mas of sys 
	--> Q = m*c*delta(T)
	--> need to measure specific heat, can't approximate
	--> calorimeter = container that prevents heat transfer
		--> calorimetry = use calorimeter to make measurements 
		--> calorimetry problem = problem where objects are thermally isolated from surroundings
			--> Q_cold + Q_hot = 0!!!

1.5 Phase Changes

	--> high pressure raises melting + boiling pt

Phase Diagrams 

	--> phase depends on temp and pressure 
	--> boiling point curve ends at critical point = above where gas and liquid stages same (supercritical fluid)
	--> pressures below triple point = no liquid, only gas or solid

Equilibrium 

	--> melting temp = solid and liquid phases in equilibrium
	--> vapor pressure equilibrium 
		--> if VP of liquid > total ambient pressure, liquid evaporates (boils)
		--> boiling point of liquid at given pressure = temp when VP = ambient pressure

Phase Change and Latent Heat 

	--> add heat during melting and boiling, but temp doesn't change bc need energy to melt solid (break bonds)
	--> heat of fusion = energy req to change solid to liquid (or released)
		--> Q = m*L_f --> L_f (latent heat fusion, cst)
	--> heat of vaporization = nrg for liquid to vapor
		--> Q = m*L_v --> L_v (latent heat of vaporization, cst)
	--> phase changes can help stabilize temperatures when they're not near melting and boiling points

1.6 Mechanisms of Heat Transfer

	--> conduction = heat transfer through physical contact 
	--> convection = heat transfer throuh macroscopic fluid movement
	--> radiation = microwaves, IR, visible, or other EMR emitted/absorbed

Conduction 

	--> 2 bodies, 1 hot and 1 cold
	--> hot has higher KE, so when they collide, cold recieves some of its KE and hot gives some away 
	--> change in temp = temp hot - temp codl 
		--> +heat transfer proportional to cross sectional area 
		--> +material that heat is transferring through affects conduction rate
	--> rate of conductive heat transfer: P = dQ/dt - [k(A(T_h - T_c)]/d
		--> P = power / rate of heat transfer (watts, Kcal per sec)
		--> A = surface area
		--> d = thickness 
		--> k = thermal conductivity of the material heat travelling thru 
	--> for insulation: small k, big d = good
		--> d/k = R factor = large for good insulator 
		--> rate of conductive heat transfer inversely proportional to R 
	--> conduction cased by random motion of atoms + molecules 
		--> ineffecive for macroscopic distances + short times

Convection 

	--> convection = for large scale matter flow
		--> forced convection = flow driven by fans/pumps 
		--> free/natural convection = flow driven by buoyant forces (hot rise, cold sink)
			--> ocean, atmospheric ciculations
	--> more complicated than conduction
		--> convection rate about proportional to temp difference 
	--> rate of convection determined by amount of free air space 
	--> usually faster than conduction

Radiation 

	--> don't need medium for EM waves to propogate 
	--> energy depends on wavelength (shorter wavelength/higher frequency = higher nrg)
	--> rate of ehat transfer depends on color:
		--> black most effective b/c absorbs better (ideal absorber)
	--> rate of heat transfer by emitted radiation: P = omega*A*e*T^4
		--> omega = 5.68x10^-8
		--> proportional to 4th power of absolute temp 
		--> can find net by subtracting 4th powers of temp 1 and temp 2
